Nov 2013

UW INFO 343  —  geometry : flocking

This project was completed for the UW INFO 343 class, Web Technologies. We were told to make an interactive game using plain JS (no third-party libraries or frameworks). I had been considering simulating bird flocking patterns, and used this assignment as an opportunity.

Thanks to Craig Reynolds and his work with Boids, I learned that basic bird flocking can be distilled into three rules: seperation (steer to avoid crowding local flockmates), alignment (steer towards the average heading of local flockmates), and cohesion (steer to move toward the average position of local flockmates).

I used HTML/CSS to draw the bird dots, and I used JS to create the bird elements and steer them around the screen (using 2D vector math). Clicking the cursor toggles through neutral, attract, and repel.

Sidenote: if the birds are forming an infinite river of death, you can zoom out your browser window to give them more room.

Nov 2012

[ personal ]  —  geometry : connect the dots

Connect the dots came about while I was experimenting with geometry using the PaperJS library, and exploring patterns within a figure known as the Flower of Life.

In nature, packed circles tend to form hexagons (the densest possible configuration). I packed a screen with circles and allowed creating connections between their centers with clicking and/or dragging. It's essentially a simple polygon drawing tool on an isometric grid.

Sidenote: zoom your browser in/out and refresh to draw a new grid at any size.

Feb 2012 - May 2012

ground(ctrl)  —  web app : RSRV

The RSRV app was an internal project for ground(ctrl). I had been learning PHP on top of my JS work, and this assignment was given to me as a chance to build my own app front to back, both server-side PHP and client-side JS simultaneously.

RSRV is a room reservation system for ground(ctrl), BKWLD, and Artery Recordings, who had all leased a building together. Between the three companies there were 4 conference rooms—some shared and some exclusive to one company or another. We wanted employees to be able to book the appropriate rooms, invite guests to meetings, all of the standard event management features.

Why didn't we use an existing solution? We also wanted an iPad as a kiosk outside each room, showing whether the room was currently available, the schedule for the day, and a quick event creation tool for reserving on the fly.

I used the CodeIgniter PHP framework with a MySQL database for the backend and the Bootstrap framework with vanilla JS modules for the front end UI. The iPads ran a heavy-client mobile web site that talked to the backend through a JSON API.

ground(ctrl)  —  mobile apps

The bulk of my time at ground(ctrl) was spent developing mobile applications with JavaScript in the Titanium framework. But, to my eternal regret, I wasn't thinking about portfolios and didn't collect screenshots of any of my mobile work. As of now, most apps have been upgraded or deprecated since I first built them, and my work can no longer be found. I can still talk a bit about them.

I built 4 major mobile apps during my employment:

ground(ctrl) app - An app for all of the clients using ground(ctrl)'s fansite service, this app had a user dashboard (with private messages, friend requests, wall comments, and merchandise order history), event list, news list, and media galleries. There were also financial reports and willcall lists for admin-level users.

FFFest app 2011 & 2012 - An app for fans attending the music festival Fun Fun Fun Fest in Austin, TX. It had daily schedules (including a personal schedule builder), festival information, ticket purchases, and of course a taco truck locator.

Queue app - An app for another aspect of ground(ctrl)'s business, venue management. The Queue app allowed venues to track their event finances live, manage guest and willcall lists, and even scan tickets into the event. This app was used at the FFFFest gates and had over 14,000 succesful scans in 2011 and 16,000 scans in 2012. This app was particularly thrilling for me, because I flew down to the event and managed the volunteers scanning at the entrance gate. It was terrifying to see crowds of people all relying on my app to let them in, but deeply gratifying when we pulled it off.

LPU app - Similar to the general ground(ctrl) app, but rebuilt to be for a single artist. The app was developed as a framework, so that it could be re-used for any clients who wanted their own custom apps. And it had one of those side-sliding menu drawers that were all the rage.

I was hired with minimal JS experience, but they were willing to give me the time to learn. I was lucky to have the help of senior developers Robert Reinhard (who was overseeing my work) and Vincent Franco (who was building backend APIs for me to use) to answer my development questions and help guide me in the right direction.
